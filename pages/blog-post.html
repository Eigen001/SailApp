The Perfect Overnight: Oyster Bay to Block Island and Back
By James Whitfield, Broadside · May 14, 2025 · 8 min read · ⚓ Oyster Bay, NY
Share:

Ninety miles round-trip, two sunrises at sea, one Great Salt Pond anchorage, and the kind of satisfaction that only comes from a well-executed offshore passage. Here's how to do it right.

94 nm
One way
14–18h
Typical passage
SW 12–18
Ideal conditions

Why Block Island is worth it

There are closer harbors. Sag Harbor is an hour's sail. Mystic, with the tide right, is a civilized day trip. But Block Island is different. It sits 13 miles offshore in the open Atlantic, and getting there requires you to actually sail — not motor in flat water, not duck behind a point when the breeze picks up, but actually navigate the Rhode Island Sound and make an offshore landfall. That commitment is most of what makes it special.

Great Salt Pond is one of the finest natural anchorages on the northeastern seaboard. It holds hundreds of boats in settled conditions, the holding is excellent in sand and mud, and the town — reachable by bike, dinghy, or the five-minute walk to New Harbor — is genuinely charming in a way that many famous destinations are not. The sunset from the anchorage, with the silhouettes of a hundred masts, is the kind of thing that makes you feel lucky to sail.

The Route at a Glance
  • Depart Oyster Bay on a flood tide, aim for The Race at max ebb + 2 hours
  • Clear Race Rock to starboard, set course 090° for Block Island SW Point
  • Final approach: round Southwest Point, enter Great Salt Pond via New Harbor channel
  • Anchor in 8–12 feet on the north side of the pond; clear of the ferry channel

Timing the passage — the key to everything

The Long Island Sound is a tidal machine. Water floods from the west through The Race at the eastern end, and everything about a Block Island passage depends on working with it. The Race — the narrow passage between Fisher's Island and the North Fork — runs at 2–4 knots depending on the phase. At springs, you'll see 4.5 on the nose if you get it wrong. Plan to arrive at The Race approximately 2 hours after maximum ebb to carry the last of the ebb and pick up the building flood through the Race itself.

Departing Oyster Bay at 2200 on a Friday night typically puts you through The Race around 0300–0400, which is ideal. You'll cross Rhode Island Sound in the early morning, watch the sun rise off the port bow somewhere southeast of Point Judith, and pick up the Block Island cliffs in the early morning light. There is almost nothing better.

"I've done this passage seventeen times. I've never once regretted staying overnight instead of day-tripping. The extra six hours of sailing is not a cost — it's the whole point."

Weather windows — don't rush this

Rhode Island Sound can be uncomfortable when southwest or south winds build against an ebb tide. The wave period shortens, the sea steepens, and what starts as a manageable 2-foot chop becomes a 4-foot mess of cross-seas. The passage is not dangerous in these conditions for a well-found yacht, but it's unpleasant. Wait for a proper window: southwest 10–18 with less than one meter of existing swell. That's your passage. You'll know it when you see it.

Check NOAA's Coastal Forecast for Southern Rhode Island waters. Pay attention to the swell period, not just the height. A 4-second, 2-foot swell is far rougher than an 8-second, 3-foot swell. The longer the period, the more comfortable the ride.

What to Check Before You Go
  • NOAA forecast: Southern Rhode Island Sound and Buzzards Bay zones
  • Tidal current tables for The Race (use NOAA tidal station TCW0121)
  • VHF WX-1 and WX-2 continuous on approach — listen for marine warnings
  • File a float plan with a shore contact — always, for overnight passages
  • Block Island VHF channel 9 for harbor patrol; anchorage management is active in summer

Great Salt Pond — the arrival

New Harbor entrance is well-marked and straightforward in good visibility. The approach channel is dredged to 10 feet; don't cut the starboard buoys tight — the shoaling east of the channel is aggressive. Once inside, the pond opens up and the chaos of the anchorage reveals itself: hundreds of boats in July and August, dozens of moorings available for hire, a marine fuel dock, and the famous Champlin's Marina if you want shore power and a hot shower.

We prefer to anchor rather than moor. The holding is excellent in 8–12 feet on the north side, away from the ferry wash. Set two anchors if you're staying more than one night or if the forecast shows a wind shift. The pond can get choppy in northwest winds once the front passes — not dangerous, but enough to keep you awake.

Coming home — the return passage

The return is often better than the outbound. Depart on a Sunday morning, time The Race for the late flood, and you'll have a glorious beam reach back to Oyster Bay. The Sound tends to be most beautiful in the early afternoon light coming from the southwest — golden, clear, the water that particular blue-green that only happens when the salinity is right. The Broadside community photographs each other constantly on this stretch. Check your vessel page when you get home. There's a good chance someone caught you perfectly framed against the Long Island bluffs.
